## The Imperative (concise formulation)

> **One must always try to act in accord with the thesis that one is not a law-governed creature.**

### What this means in practice

The Imperative is a directive about **trying**, not a theoretical proof that free will exists.

Trying to act in accord with the thesis of free will includes:

- treating one’s own present volitional effort as genuinely open,
- refusing to regard that effort as merely a law-governed event,
- and, when the issue arises, **affirming and defending the libertarian thesis**.

In this sense, **trying to act as free also includes trying to believe that one is free**.

This does not mean claiming to _know_ that free will exists.
Rather, it means that the act of trying itself is undertaken **in accordance with the thesis that one is free**, and therefore the agent attempts to adopt that thesis as true.

Because the Imperative applies only to **one’s own present act of trying**, it does not logically compel claims about other minds or about the metaphysical structure of the universe.
It governs only how one must engage in **this act of volitional effort now**.

## The Core Lemma (the whole argument in one place)

1. Consider **only this present act**: _my trying, here and now_.

2. There are exactly two live possibilities about it:
   - **N (nomic):** this trying is wholly law-governed (deterministic and/or probabilistic) at some fundamental level.
   - **F (Free):** this trying is not law-governed at any fundamental level (libertarian Free-Volitional).

3. The question is **practical, not theoretical**:
   not “Which is true?” but **“How must I engage in this trying, as a truth-seeker, under this uncertainty?”**

4. Use one asymmetry:
   - If an error is **avoidable**, a truth-seeker must try to avoid it.
   - If an error is **unavoidable**, there is nothing one can be required to try to avoid.

5. Compare two stances toward the thesis in **this present trying**:
   - **T:** try to act **in accord with the thesis** that this trying is not law-governed (treat it as Free-Volitional).
   - **¬T:** do not try to act in accord with that thesis (e.g., treat the trying as law-governed, refrain from the thesis in practice, or attempt practical agnosticism).

6. If **F** is true, then **¬T** is an **avoidable misalignment with truth**
   (because in F I could have tried to engage in my action according to the true thesis).

   Therefore truth-seeking requires **T**.

7. If **N** is true, then both **T** and **¬T** are **unavoidable outcomes** of the nomic causal order.

   Therefore adopting **T** cannot be condemned as a failure to avoid avoidable untruth — because nothing else was possible.

8. Therefore, under truth-seeking, the only stance that is correct if **F** and not action-guidingly refutable if **N** is:

> **Always try to act in accord with the thesis that you are not a law-governed creature.**

9. **Scope restriction (crucial):**
   this lemma justifies **only** the present first-person attempt.
   It does **not** by itself justify claims about your other acts, the distant future, or other minds.

## Five mental substitutions that make the Imperative look wrong

> **If you find yourself thinking one of the following, you have already shifted the problem.**
> The Libertarian Imperative is often misunderstood because the mind automatically replaces its narrow target with a more familiar question.

### Accordion: Common substitutions that misread the target

#### 1) Replacing **trying-now** with **belief-in-general**

- **What it feels like:**
  “But is the claim true? What evidence do we have? What metaphysical theory supports it?”

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Imperative is not a command about belief. It is a directive about **trying** under uncertainty.

- **Correct target:**
  The question is not _“What should I believe about free will?”_ but
  **“What must I try to do now if I seek truth?”**

---

#### 2) Replacing **first-person dominance** with **public debate victory**

- **What it feels like:**
  “But determinists will disagree with you. Doesn’t that cancel your conclusion?”

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Imperative is not designed to defeat other people’s philosophical positions.

- **Correct target:**
  It asks only:
  **What must _I_ try to do, here and now, if I seek truth under the N/F uncertainty?**

---

#### 3) Replacing **“could have done otherwise in the same situation”** with **“would have done otherwise if earlier conditions were different”**

- **What it feels like:**
  “If you had tried harder to understand determinism, you would have realised the world is nomic and would not have proposed this imperative.”

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  This argument quietly changes the conditions.
  It imagines a different past (“if you had tried harder”) and then infers a different result.

- **Correct target:**
  The Imperative asks whether the agent could have acted differently **given exactly the same total situation and laws**.
  In a fully nomic world the next state is fixed by the current state and the laws, so the agent **could not have tried otherwise in that same situation**, even though different earlier causes would have produced a different outcome.

---

#### 4) Replacing **narrowly logical self-reference** with a **mechanistic theory**

- **What it feels like:**
  “Explain the physical mechanism of free will first. Otherwise the argument is incomplete.”

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Imperative is not a theory of how free will works.

- **Correct target:**
  It is a **self-referential practical rule** for how to engage in the present act of trying under the uncertainty between nomic and non-nomic functioning.

---

#### 5) Replacing **non-game-theoretic logic** with **wager psychology**

- **What it feels like:**
  “This is basically Pascal’s Wager or William James’ ‘will to believe.’”

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  Pascal’s Wager weighs gains and losses and depends on probabilities and payoffs.

- **Correct target:**
  The Libertarian Imperative does **not** weigh gains or losses.
  Its conclusion follows from a **logical asymmetry between avoidable and unavoidable wrongness** in a uniquely self-referential situation:
  one is asked to decide whether one can decide.

## Common objections and misunderstandings (from real life)

_(Drawn from actual correspondence with philosophers, editors, and referees. Each item links the misread to the specific substitution above.)_

### Accordion A — Objections (as they actually appear)

#### A1) “If determinism holds, both you and the determinist are unavoidable, so your conclusion doesn’t follow.”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “The libertarian believes that he has free will.
  The determinist believes that no one has free will.
  If determinism is true, then both beliefs are unavoidable.
  Therefore the claim that **‘the fallacious free will thesis could not have been avoided by anybody’** does not follow.”

- **What actually happened:**
  This treats **unavoidable wrongness under determinism** as if it were an objection.

- **Why it’s a misreading:**
  The Imperative explicitly relies on this very point.
  If the world is nomic, then any mistaken belief about free will is **unavoidable**, and therefore not something a truth-seeker could have been required to avoid.

- **Correct understanding:**
  The Imperative does **not** claim that others must reach the same conclusion.
  It only states that, for one’s own present trying, the stance
  _“act as non-nomic”_ is:
  - **truth-aligned if freedom is real**, and
  - **unavoidably wrong if nomism is true**.

  That asymmetry is exactly what the argument depends on.

#### A2) “You’re stuck in infinite regress: the decision to try is caused unless it’s free…”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “You describe a situation in which you try to behave free-volitionally, with apparent success.
  But this decision to behave that way (together with other factors) could itself be part of a deterministic process that causes the behaviour.”

- **What actually happened:**
  The objection demands a **causal explanation of the trying itself**.

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Libertarian Imperative does not claim that the present act of trying is already known to be free.
  On the contrary, the whole argument explicitly allows two possibilities:
  - the trying is **nomic**, or
  - the trying is **Free-Volitional**.

- **Correct understanding:**
  The Imperative is not a theory explaining how free acts work.
  It is a **practical rule about how to engage in the present act of trying under this uncertainty**.

  If the trying is in fact nomic, then trying to act as non-nomic is simply one more nomic event in the causal chain.
  If the trying is Free-Volitional, then the same act may succeed as a genuine free act.

  The argument therefore does not require resolving the causal status of the trying beforehand.

#### A3) “Even if determinism is true, the false belief might have been avoidable — you could have thought harder.”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “Even if the world is deterministic, it may still be true that the mistaken belief could have been avoided.
  For example, if the person had thought harder about the arguments, he would not have held the false belief.”

- **What actually happened:**
  The objection silently changes the conditions of the situation.
  It imagines a different earlier state of the agent (“if he had thought harder”) and then concludes that the mistake was avoidable.

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Libertarian Imperative uses a stricter notion of avoidability:
  whether the agent **could have done otherwise given exactly the same total situation and the same laws of nature**.

  In a nomic world the total state of the world together with its laws uniquely determines the next state.
  Therefore the agent could not have “thought harder” unless the preceding causes had been different.

- **Correct understanding:**
  If the world is nomic and the agent in fact formed the belief in free will, then that belief was **nomically inevitable**.
  Saying “he could have avoided it if he had thought harder” only describes what would happen **under different earlier conditions**, not what the agent could have done in the same situation.

  The Imperative therefore treats such an error as **unavoidable** in a nomic world — which is exactly the premise the argument relies on.

#### A4) “This looks like Pascal’s Wager or William James’ ‘will to believe.’”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “Your reasoning seems similar to Pascal’s Wager or to William James’ view that believing in free will can be justified on pragmatic or psychological grounds.”

- **What actually happened:**
  The Imperative is interpreted as a **pragmatic wager**: choose the belief that has the better consequences or psychological benefits.

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  Pascal’s Wager is a **game-theoretical argument**.
  It weighs gains and losses and depends on estimates of probabilities and payoffs.

  The Libertarian Imperative does nothing of this kind.
  Its conclusion does **not** depend on the size of gains or losses, or on any probability assignments.

- **Correct understanding:**
  The Imperative rests on a **purely logical asymmetry** between two cases:
  - If freedom is real, failing to act as free is an **avoidable misalignment with truth**.
  - If the world is nomic, any mistaken belief in freedom is **unavoidable**.

  Because the situation is **self-referential** (one must decide whether one can decide), this asymmetry yields a dominance rule about what one must try.

  No appeal to expected benefits, psychological comfort, or practical payoffs is involved.

#### A5) “So the point is that we have a moral duty to act as if we are free agents.”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “You are not trying to prove that we are free.
  Rather, you are arguing that we have a **moral obligation** to act as if we are free agents.”

- **What actually happened:**
  The Imperative is interpreted as a **moral principle about responsibility and ethical conduct**.

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  Acting as a responsible moral agent may indeed presuppose freedom.
  But the Libertarian Imperative does **not** derive its force from ethics, accountability, or the requirements of moral life.

- **Correct understanding:**
  The Imperative is grounded in a **truth-seeking constraint on one’s own present act of trying**.

  The reasoning is purely logical:
  - If one is in fact a Free agent, failing to act as free would be an **avoidable misalignment with truth**.
  - If one is nomically determined, any mistaken libertarian stance is **unavoidable**.

  Because truth-seeking requires avoiding **avoidable error**, the rational rule is to try to act in accordance with the thesis that one is not law-governed.

  Moral responsibility may follow if freedom is real, but it is **not the basis of the Imperative itself**.

#### A6) “Free will might still operate within probabilistic natural laws.”

- **What it sounds like:**
  “Even if determinism is false, free will might still be compatible with probabilistic causal laws of nature.
  So the claim that free action must be non-nomic seems unnecessarily strong.”

- **What actually happened:**
  The discussion shifts from the **logic of the Imperative** to a debate about **which libertarian metaphysical theory of free will is correct**.

- **Why it’s a shift:**
  The Libertarian Imperative is not trying to settle the metaphysical question of **how free will works**.
  Its logical target is a specific doctrine: **nomism** — the claim that all processes in the world are governed by universal deterministic or probabilistic laws of dynamics.

- **Correct understanding:**
  The formulation “act in accordance with the thesis that one is **not a law-governed creature**” is intentionally **Anti-Nomic**.

  It is designed to reject the assumption that one’s volitional activity is completely governed by universal laws of nature.

  This does not attempt to rule out every conceivable theory of free agency.
  It simply denies the thesis that our behaviour is **entirely law-governed**, which is the key premise behind the standard arguments against free will.
